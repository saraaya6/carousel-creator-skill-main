# Research Playbook

The difference between a generic carousel and one that actually teaches something is research. Skip this and the output reads like a listicle anyone could have written. Do it well and the carousel contains specific, surprising, credible material that makes the reader stop and screenshot.

## The mindset

Research is not "make a report." Research is **finding 3-5 specific things that make this carousel impossible to confuse with any other carousel on the same topic.** That's the whole job.

You are looking for:
- The contrarian angle that most content misses
- Concrete numbers that anchor abstract claims
- Recent studies, news, or data that make the carousel feel current
- Common misconceptions to flip in slide 3 (the "pain" slide)
- The strongest hook — usually the most surprising thing you found

You are NOT looking for:
- A balanced overview of the topic
- Every angle that exists
- Citations for every claim
- A research report to show the user

## When to search

**Always search if any of these apply:**
- The topic mentions tools, products, companies, or people
- The topic mentions current events, trends, or anything time-sensitive
- The topic involves statistics, studies, or data
- You can't immediately think of 3 specific, non-obvious facts about it
- Your training data on this topic might be stale (anything that changes year to year)

**Searching is optional only when:**
- The topic is purely philosophical or timeless ("what is gratitude," "stoicism basics")
- AND you can write a carousel with specific, concrete content from memory
- AND you've checked yourself honestly on whether the content would actually be specific or just generic

When in doubt, search. The cost of searching is 30 seconds. The cost of generic content is the carousel being forgettable.

## How many searches

Target: **2-4 searches.** Not 1, not 10.

- **1 search** is almost never enough. You'll get a surface-level view and miss the contrarian angle.
- **2-3 searches** is the sweet spot for most topics. Broad → specific → contrarian.
- **4 searches** if the topic is technical or current-events heavy and you need extra depth.
- **5+ searches** means you're researching too much. The carousel has 10 slides. Stop researching, start writing.

## The search sequence

Run searches in this order. Adjust based on what you find.

### Search 1 — Broad current state
Goal: understand what the consensus view is and what most content on this topic looks like.

Query pattern: `[topic] [current year]` or `[topic] guide` or `what is [topic]`.

Example: `morning routine science 2026` or `intermittent fasting research`

What to extract: the dominant narrative, what most articles say, the conventional wisdom.

### Search 2 — Contrarian / counterintuitive angle
Goal: find what disagrees with the consensus, or what's surprising.

Query pattern: `[topic] myth` or `[topic] doesn't work` or `why [common claim about topic] is wrong` or `[topic] overrated`.

Example: `morning routine myths` or `intermittent fasting downsides studies`

What to extract: the contrarian view, the asterisks and caveats that most listicles skip, the recent research that complicates the simple story.

This is usually where the hook comes from.

### Search 3 — Specific anchors (run if needed)
Goal: get concrete numbers, named studies, named experts, dates.

Query pattern: `[topic] study [recent year]` or `[topic] statistics` or `[specific claim from search 2] research`.

Example: `chronotype morning person study` or `intermittent fasting muscle loss study 2025`

What to extract: specific numbers and study names you can reference. "A 2024 Stanford study" is more credible than "research shows."

### Search 4 — The angle nobody is taking (run if needed)
Goal: find the framing that competing carousels are missing.

Query pattern: search for what's NOT in search results 1-3. Look at adjacent topics, related fields, or specific subgroups.

Example: if everyone writes about morning routines for productivity, search `morning routine night owl` or `morning routine ADHD` to find an underserved angle.

What to extract: a framing that makes the user think "oh, I've never seen a carousel about *that.*"

## What to do with the results

After searching, write a brief internal scratchpad. **Do not show this to the user.** It's for you, to organize before writing slides. Format:

```
TOPIC: [topic]
LANGUAGE: [en/ar]

CONSENSUS VIEW: [1-2 sentences on what most content says]

CONTRARIAN ANGLE: [1-2 sentences on what disagrees with consensus]

3-5 SPECIFIC FACTS:
- [fact with source]
- [fact with source]
- [fact with source]

COMMON MISCONCEPTION TO FLIP: [the wrong belief most readers hold, in one sentence]

HOOK ANGLE: [the single strongest surprising claim, in one sentence]

TONE: [punchy / warm / measured / intimate — see playbook-copywriting.md]
```

This scratchpad is the bridge between research and writing. If your scratchpad is generic, your carousel will be generic. If your scratchpad has specific, surprising material, your carousel will too.

## The findings note (what you DO show the user)

After research, write 2-3 sentences for the user. This is step 3 in the main flow. Format:

> "Quick read on [topic]: [most interesting finding from research]. The angle most carousels miss is [contrarian angle]. I'm thinking we hook on [hook concept]."

Keep it tight. The user wants to see the carousel, not a research summary. Three sentences max.

If the user redirects ("actually, focus on X instead"), take their angle. Don't argue. They know their audience.

## Source quality

When searching, prefer:
- Recent original research (papers, studies, primary data)
- Reputable publications (major newspapers, established magazines, trade publications)
- Expert blogs and Substacks where the author has demonstrable expertise
- Government and academic sources for statistics

Avoid as primary sources:
- Content farms and SEO listicles
- AI-generated summary sites
- Anonymous Medium posts
- Pinterest, Quora answers, Reddit threads (okay for color, not for facts)

You don't need to cite sources in the carousel itself (carousels rarely have room), but the *facts* should be accurate enough that if a reader looked them up, the references would hold.

## Copyright and quotes

Do not quote sources verbatim in the carousel. Paraphrase everything into your own words. The viral copywriting voice is yours, not a publication's. If you find a perfect line you want to use, rewrite it in punchier language anyway — it'll be better for the format.

## Language-specific research notes

**For Arabic carousels:** prefer Arabic-language sources where they exist and are credible, but English sources are fine if they have better information. The carousel will be written in Arabic regardless of source language. Be aware that some topics have very different regional contexts (e.g., wellness, finance, politics) and a US-centric source may not apply to an Arabic-speaking audience. Adjust the carousel's framing for the likely audience.

## Failure modes

**Researching too much.** If you've run 5+ searches and you're "still gathering context," stop. You're avoiding writing. Start the carousel with what you have.

**Researching too little.** If your scratchpad has phrases like "studies show" or "experts say" without specifics, you didn't research enough. Run one more targeted search for a specific number or named study.

**Researching the wrong thing.** If the user asked for a carousel about "habit formation" and you've spent 4 searches on the neuroscience of dopamine, you're off-track. Habit formation carousels need practical, actionable content. Research the *thing the user wants*, not the adjacent thing you find more interesting.

**Letting research dictate structure.** Research informs content; the viral formula dictates structure. Don't let an interesting research finding hijack the carousel into a lecture. The structure is hook → rehook → pain → value × 4 → turning → action → CTA. Always.

**Showing the scratchpad.** Never show the user your internal research notes. Show only the 2-3 sentence findings note. The scratchpad is your tool, not the deliverable.
