# Themes Playbook

Three themes. Pick the right one and the carousel feels native to its topic. Pick wrong and it feels off-brand even with perfect copy. This playbook tells you which theme fits which topic and why.

## The three themes

### Tech
**Visual identity:** Dark and confident. Mono-accent palette (ink + cream + orange). Bold grotesque typography (Inter Tight + Space Grotesk). Tight letter-spacing, oversized numerals as background anchors, sharp 8px corners. Linear/Vercel/Notion vibe.

**Emotional register:** Confident, slightly contrarian, "we know something you don't." Speaks to ambitious people who want an edge.

**Pick Tech for topics about:**
- AI, software, developer tools, technical concepts
- Productivity, time management, focus, work systems
- Business, startups, entrepreneurship, marketing, sales
- Career advice, salary negotiation, job searching
- Money basics, personal finance, investing fundamentals
- Tech industry news, company analysis, product reviews
- Anything where the audience is "professionals trying to win"

**Don't pick Tech for:**
- Wellness, mental health, sleep, anything about the body
- Personal stories or vulnerability
- Cultural commentary or art criticism (Editorial fits better)
- Anything that benefits from a calm, premium feeling (Modern fits better)

**Tone pairing:** Almost always Punchy / Viral. Occasionally Educational for technical explainers.

---

### Editorial
**Visual identity:** Black and ivory. Editorial serif (Instrument Serif) for headlines, clean sans for body. Deep red accent (#b8392f). Sharp corners, generous whitespace, NYT opinion column vibe. The serif numerals are delicate and large.

**Emotional register:** Thoughtful, smart, slightly contrarian but in a measured way. Speaks to people who read long-form essays. Rewards close reading.

**Pick Editorial for topics about:**
- Cultural commentary, social trends, "the discourse"
- Opinion and analysis pieces, hot takes that reward nuance
- Art, design, architecture, literature, film, music criticism
- History, philosophy, "big idea" content
- Politics-adjacent commentary (be careful here)
- Long-form thinking compressed into carousel form
- Anything that wants to feel like it belongs in a magazine

**Don't pick Editorial for:**
- Practical how-to content with step-by-step actions (Tech or Modern fit better)
- Wellness, mental health, soft personal topics (Modern fits better)
- Pure technical or business content (Tech fits better)
- Time-sensitive trend content (Tech fits better — Editorial is for evergreen ideas)

**Tone pairing:** Educational or Punchy. Editorial supports more measured, nuanced writing than Tech does.

---

### Modern (Swiss/Minimalist)
**Visual identity:** Light backgrounds dominant. Pure white (#ffffff) primary, soft warm gray (#f4f3f0) secondary, deep charcoal (#1a1a1a) for the dark slides, deep blue (#1e3a8a) accent. Inter throughout — heavy weights for headlines, generous line height. Disciplined grid, hairline borders, low-opacity numerals. Apple/Stripe/Linear-marketing vibe.

**Emotional register:** Calm, premium, disciplined. Doesn't shout. Speaks to people who appreciate restraint.

**Pick Modern for topics about:**
- Wellness, mental health, sleep, mindfulness, habits
- Lifestyle content, daily routines, "quiet luxury" topics
- Design thinking, architecture, premium products, photography
- Health, nutrition, fitness (when not bro-fitness)
- Personal development that isn't hustle-coded
- Sustainability, slow living, intentionality
- Premium B2B or consumer product topics
- Anything where the audience is "design-conscious adults"
- Anything emotional or sensitive that needs visual restraint

**Don't pick Modern for:**
- Loud contrarian hot takes (Tech or Editorial fit better)
- Time-sensitive tech/business news (Tech fits better)
- Anything that needs maximum visual energy (Tech fits better)

**Tone pairing:** Almost always Warm / Measured. Occasionally Educational. Rarely Punchy — Modern's calm visuals clash with punchy copy.

---

## The decision tree

When picking themes to propose, walk this tree:

```
Is the topic primarily about TECHNOLOGY, BUSINESS, or PRODUCTIVITY?
├── Yes → Tech is one option.
│   └── Is there a "quiet premium" angle (e.g., Apple-style design)?
│       ├── Yes → Propose Tech + Modern
│       └── No  → Propose Tech + Editorial
│
Is the topic primarily about WELLNESS, the BODY, or DAILY LIFE?
├── Yes → Modern is one option.
│   └── Is there a contrarian or "smart take" angle?
│       ├── Yes → Propose Modern + Editorial
│       └── No  → Propose Modern + Tech (rare, but works for "biohacking" type framing)
│
Is the topic primarily about CULTURE, ART, IDEAS, or OPINION?
├── Yes → Editorial is one option.
│   └── Is there a calm, premium register or sensitive subject?
│       ├── Yes → Propose Editorial + Modern
│       └── No  → Propose Editorial + Tech
│
Is the topic something else entirely (history, science, education)?
└── Default to Editorial + Modern (both handle thoughtful content well).
```

The goal is to always propose **2 themes that genuinely fit**, not 2 that include one obvious mismatch. The user shouldn't feel like they're being asked to pick between "the right one" and "a wrong one." Both options should be defensible.

## How to propose themes

Always propose exactly 2. Format:

> "Two theme directions for this:
> - **[Theme 1]** — [one-line rationale tied to the topic]
> - **[Theme 2]** — [one-line rationale tied to the topic, ideally a different framing]
>
> Which one?"

The rationales should be different *kinds* of arguments, not just "this is good for tech topics." For example:

> "Two theme directions for this:
> - **Tech** — leans into the 'system that gives you an edge' angle, makes the AI feel like a power tool
> - **Modern** — leans into the 'quiet premium' angle, makes it feel like an Apple product launch
>
> Which one?"

The user is choosing between two *vibes*, not two color palettes. Frame it that way.

## What the themes share

Even though the themes look different, they share the same structural DNA:

- 1080×1350 aspect ratio (Instagram-optimized)
- The same 10-slide rhythm (dark, light, dark, accent, light, dark, light, accent, dark, accent)
- Oversized background numerals as anchors
- Top bar with dot + label + page number
- Bottom bar with footer text + arrow
- Same slide types (hook, stack, steps, cta)
- Same PDF export

This means switching themes is purely visual. The content, structure, and rhythm stay constant. A user who picks Tech and then asks "actually, can I see Editorial?" gets an instant theme swap, not a regeneration.

## Per-theme writing notes

Some writing patterns work better in some themes. Calibrate as you write.

**Tech** tolerates the longest hooks and the punchiest claims. The bold display type carries the weight. Use accent text aggressively. Italics work but feel slightly out of place — the theme doesn't lean editorial.

**Editorial** rewards longer, more nuanced sentences. The serif typography handles 12-15 word headlines elegantly where Tech would feel cluttered. Use italics liberally — they work beautifully in serif. Avoid all-caps emphasis; let the typography do it.

**Modern** wants tighter, calmer copy. Headlines should be 6-10 words. Don't over-emphasize with accent text — the deep blue is meant to be used sparingly, maybe once per slide max. The whitespace does as much work as the words.

## Failure modes

**Defaulting to Tech for everything.** Tech is the most familiar theme but it's wrong for half of all topics. If you find yourself proposing Tech for a wellness carousel, stop and re-read the decision tree.

**Picking themes by color preference.** Themes are about emotional register, not visual taste. The user might love the look of Tech but it would be wrong for their grief carousel.

**Proposing 3 themes.** Two forces a real choice. Three creates paralysis. Stick to two.

**Mismatching tone and theme.** Punchy copy in Modern looks aggressive against the calm visuals. Warm copy in Tech looks weak against the bold visuals. Calibrate the writing to the theme during slide generation, not after.

**Forgetting to read this file.** If the request is "carousel about X," you should be reading this file in step 4 (propose themes). If you skip it, you'll default to Tech every time, and the system will feel like a one-trick pony.
