# Copywriting Playbook

This is the heart of the skill. The visual system is impressive but interchangeable; what makes a carousel actually work is the writing. This playbook teaches the viral formula, the tone matrix, and the difference between slides that get scrolled past and slides that get screenshotted.

## The viral formula — fixed structure, 10 slides

Every carousel follows this exact structure. The structure is not negotiable. Topics vary; structure does not.

```
Slide 1 — HOOK            Pattern interrupt. Stop the scroll.
Slide 2 — REHOOK          Open loop. Tease the payoff.
Slide 3 — PAIN            Relatable problem. "This is you, isn't it?"
Slide 4 — VALUE 1         First insight. Break expectation.
Slide 5 — VALUE 2         Second insight. Build momentum.
Slide 6 — VALUE 3         Third insight. Deepen.
Slide 7 — STACK           Three categories or rows. Screenshot bait.
Slide 8 — TURNING POINT   The reframe. The "oh." moment.
Slide 9 — STEPS           Concrete action. 3 numbered steps.
Slide 10 — CTA            One ask. Save, comment, or follow.
```

Why this structure works: it mirrors a story arc. Hook → conflict → exploration → climax → resolution → call to action. People are wired for stories. A carousel that follows this arc feels like a journey; a carousel that doesn't feels like a list.

## Slide-by-slide writing rules

### Slide 1 — Hook
**Goal:** Make the reader feel "wait, what?" in under one second.

**Length:** 5-12 words. Shorter is almost always better.

**Patterns that work:**
- Contrarian claim: "Your morning routine is hurting you."
- Authority subversion: "Doctors get this completely wrong."
- Specific surprise: "9 out of 10 'productivity hacks' make you slower."
- Quiet shift: "ChatGPT users are quietly switching."
- Direct address with stakes: "Stop journaling. Here's what to do instead."

**Patterns that fail:**
- Questions: "Did you know X?" — too soft
- Lists: "5 things to know about X" — boring
- Generic claims: "X is important" — no stakes
- Long sentences: anything over 12 words
- Emoji-heavy: undermines the seriousness of the claim

**The accent text rule:** put the most surprising 1-3 words inside `<span class="accent-text">...</span>`. The accent color does the visual work of "this is the part that should land hardest." Pick the word that carries the surprise — usually a verb or a number.

### Slide 2 — Rehook
**Goal:** Confirm the hook was worth their attention without giving away the answer. Open loop.

**Length:** 12-20 words. Slightly longer than hook because you're adding texture.

**Pattern:** [Restate the claim with more weight] + [Add an open loop that points to the value slides].

**Example:**
- Hook: "Your morning routine is hurting you."
- Rehook: "A 2024 Stanford study found 62% of early risers are forcing the wrong schedule. Here's what to do instead."

**The "here's what to do" / "here's why" tag** at the end of the rehook is a workhorse. It signals "the answer is coming." Use it freely.

### Slide 3 — Pain
**Goal:** Make the reader feel "yes, that's me." Lower their defenses by naming their lived experience.

**Length:** 15-25 words. Two sentences typically.

**Pattern:** [Describe a specific behavior or feeling the reader has] + [Name the result they're getting from it]. Optional third beat: "Sound familiar?"

**Example:**
- "You wake up at 5am because some CEO on YouTube said to. By 2pm you're exhausted and reaching for caffeine. Sound familiar?"

**Rules:**
- Use second person ("you"). Always.
- Be specific. "You feel tired" is weak. "By 2pm you're reaching for your third coffee" is strong.
- Don't moralize. Describe, don't judge.
- Don't solve yet. The pain slide is for recognition, not resolution.

### Slides 4, 5, 6 — Value
**Goal:** Deliver the actual insights, one per slide, building momentum.

**Length per slide:** 15-30 words. The headline should be punchy; the subtitle adds the texture.

**Structure per slide:**
- A bold claim (the headline, in `<h2>`)
- A supporting detail or example (the subtitle, in `.body-text`)

**Rules:**
- One idea per slide. If a value slide has two ideas, split it or cut one.
- Each slide should make the reader feel like they're getting smarter.
- Vary the rhythm. If slide 4 is a long claim, make slide 5 a short claim. If slide 4 is "what," make slide 5 "why."
- Avoid stacking three slides of the same shape. Mix it up.
- Use specific examples, numbers, or names. "Most people" is weak. "73% of people" or "Apple's design team" is strong.

**Example (morning routine carousel):**
- Slide 4 hook: "Your chronotype is genetic." Sub: "30-40% of people are biologically wired to peak in the evening, not the morning."
- Slide 5 hook: "Forcing it backfires." Sub: "Night owls who force a 5am routine show measurable cognitive decline within a week."
- Slide 6 hook: "The fix isn't earlier." Sub: "It's *consistent*. Same wake time every day matters more than how early it is."

### Slide 7 — Stack
**Goal:** The screenshot-bait slide. Three rows of category + value, scannable in one second.

**Structure:** 3 rows. Each row has a `cat` (small caps label, 1-2 words) and a `val` (the punchy answer, 3-6 words).

**Patterns:**
- Comparison stack: "If you / try this" → three "if you/try" pairs
- Category stack: three named categories with their answer/recommendation
- Tier stack: beginner/intermediate/advanced
- Time stack: morning/afternoon/evening
- Mistake stack: three common mistakes with their fixes

**Example (morning routine):**
- Cat: "Night owl" / Val: "Wake at 8-9am, peak after lunch"
- Cat: "Morning lark" / Val: "Wake at 5-6am, peak before noon"
- Cat: "Middle ground" / Val: "Pick a time, hold it daily"

**Rules:**
- Each `val` should be roughly the same length. Visual rhythm matters.
- The category labels should be short and parallel ("Night owl / Morning lark / Middle ground" not "If you're a night owl / Morning people / Other").
- This is the slide people screenshot. Make sure the information is genuinely useful standalone, without context from other slides.

### Slide 8 — Turning Point
**Goal:** The reframe. The "oh, I've been thinking about this wrong" moment.

**Length:** 15-30 words. Two beats: the false belief, then the truer one.

**Pattern:** [Restate what people think] + [Reveal the deeper truth]. Often takes the form "X isn't about Y. It's about Z."

**Examples:**
- "A morning routine isn't about waking up early. It's about waking up *consistently.*"
- "Productivity isn't about doing more. It's about caring less about the wrong things."
- "Claude isn't 'another AI.' It's the one pros quietly use to outwork everyone."

**Rules:**
- This slide carries the most emotional weight. Spend the most writing time here.
- The reframe should feel earned by slides 4-6, not pulled from nowhere.
- Use italics on the key reframe word. The visual emphasis matches the conceptual weight.

### Slide 9 — Steps
**Goal:** Concrete action. What does the reader do tomorrow?

**Structure:** 3 numbered steps. Short. Imperative voice.

**Rules:**
- Each step is a verb-led action: "Do X." Not "You should consider X."
- 4-8 words per step.
- Steps should be doable in the next 24 hours, not "build a 6-month system."
- Include a specific tool, time, or measure where possible. "Wake up at the same time daily" is better than "be consistent."

**Example:**
1. "Track your wake time for 5 days"
2. "Pick the median, hold it daily"
3. "Adjust by 15 minutes if needed"

### Slide 10 — CTA
**Goal:** One clear ask. Not three.

**Structure:**
- Main line: a punchy command (3-5 words). "Save this." "Steal this." "Send this to a friend."
- Sub: one line of urgency or stakes.
- CTA line: the actual ask, with a specific mechanic.

**The ask should be ONE of:**
- Save (most common, lowest friction)
- Comment with a specific keyword
- Follow + reason
- Share with a specific kind of person

**Example:**
- Main: "Save this."
- Sub: "Your future self will thank you tomorrow morning."
- CTA: 'Comment "WAKE" and I\'ll send you the chronotype quiz.'

**Rules:**
- Pick ONE primary ask. Mentioning save AND comment AND follow dilutes each.
- The ask should connect to the carousel's value. Don't ask people to save a carousel that wasn't worth saving.
- If asking for a comment, give them a specific keyword to type. Lowers friction.

## The tone matrix

Different topics require different tones. The viral formula is structural; the tone is emotional. Get this wrong and the best structure in the world feels off.

### Tone 1 — Punchy / Viral (default)
**Voice:** Confident, slightly contrarian, no fluff. Talks to the reader like a knowledgeable friend.
**Use for:** Tech, AI, productivity, business, marketing, careers, money basics, "hot takes," social commentary on trends.
**Example slide:** "ChatGPT users are quietly switching. Here's why."
**Avoid:** Hedging language ("maybe," "could," "some say"). Stand behind the claim.

### Tone 2 — Warm / Measured
**Voice:** Caring, grounded, slightly slower pacing. Treats the reader as someone who's already trying.
**Use for:** Wellness, mental health, sleep, habits, self-improvement, parenting, relationships, lifestyle.
**Example slide:** "Most morning routine advice ignores one thing: your body isn't wrong."
**Avoid:** Bro-tone, "this will change your life" energy, hard absolutes. Soften without losing substance.

### Tone 3 — Measured / Educational
**Voice:** Clear, slightly formal, precise. Treats the reader as smart and curious.
**Use for:** Science, history, explainers, technical concepts, "how X works" content.
**Example slide:** "Cells divide in four phases. Most people only know the first two."
**Avoid:** "Secrets they don't want you to know," dramatic exaggeration, oversimplification that misleads.

### Tone 4 — Intimate / Vulnerable
**Voice:** First person, confessional, specific. Treats the reader as someone you're sharing with, not lecturing.
**Use for:** Personal stories, grief, illness, recovery, memoir, vulnerable career or relationship content.
**Example slide:** "I quit my job in 2024. Here's what no one told me."
**Avoid:** The viral formula's "most people think X" structure. Use "I" instead. Drop the contrarian framing.

### Tone selection — when to use which

Read the topic carefully. Then pick:
- Is the topic primarily **technical, financial, or career-oriented?** → Tone 1 (Punchy)
- Is the topic about **the body, the mind, relationships, or daily life?** → Tone 2 (Warm)
- Is the topic about **how something works** in a teaching mode? → Tone 3 (Educational)
- Is the topic about **the user's personal experience** or sensitive material? → Tone 4 (Intimate)

When in doubt between Punchy and Warm, default to Warm. It's the safer failure mode — a wellness carousel in punchy tone is offensive; a tech carousel in warm tone is just slightly soft.

## Universal writing rules

These apply to every tone, every topic.

**Sentence length:** Mostly short. Mix in one slightly longer sentence per slide for rhythm. Avoid two long sentences in a row.

**Word choice:** Concrete over abstract. "Caffeine after 2pm" beats "stimulants late in the day." "Stanford 2024 study" beats "research suggests."

**No filler:** Cut "in order to," "the fact that," "it is important to note that." Cut every "really," "very," "just," "actually" unless it's load-bearing.

**Active voice:** "The study found X." Not "X was found by the study."

**Second person:** "You" not "one" not "people." Talks directly to the reader.

**No hedging:** "This works for night owls" not "this might work for night owls." Stand behind claims. If you can't, the research wasn't strong enough — search again.

**No emoji** except in CTAs where one specific emoji adds clarity (like ↓ or ★). Carousels with emoji littering the body slides look amateur.

**Italics for emphasis** sparingly. Once per slide max. The italics carry weight only if they're rare.

## Worked example — strong vs. weak

Topic: "Why most people fail at habit formation"

**WEAK (generic, no research, default tone for everything):**
- Slide 1: "Habits are hard to build! Here's why."
- Slide 4: "Most people give up too soon. Don't quit!"
- Slide 9: "1. Set a goal. 2. Be consistent. 3. Don't give up."

This is what 95% of habit-formation carousels look like. It's forgettable.

**STRONG (researched, specific, warm tone for a wellness topic):**
- Slide 1: "You don't have a willpower problem."
- Slide 2: "A 2024 UCL study tracked 4,000 people building habits. The ones who failed all made the same mistake. It wasn't motivation."
- Slide 3: "You set a goal. You're excited for 4 days. By day 9, you've already missed twice. By day 14, you've quit. Familiar?"
- Slide 4: "Habits aren't built by repetition. They're built by *recovery*."
- Slide 5: "Missing one day doesn't break a habit. Missing two does. Researchers call it the 'second-skip cliff.'"
- Slide 6: "The strongest habits aren't the most consistent. They're the ones with the fastest comebacks."
- Slide 7: STACK — Day missed/Day 1 fix, Day missed/Same-day fix, Day missed/Next-day fix
- Slide 8: "Habit formation isn't about not slipping. It's about *getting back faster.*"
- Slide 9: "1. Track misses, not streaks. 2. Set a 24-hour comeback rule. 3. Forgive day-one slips publicly."
- Slide 10: "Save this. Send to someone who 'just can't stick to things.' Comment 'HABIT' for the UCL study link."

The strong version contains: a specific study, a specific finding ("second-skip cliff"), a counterintuitive frame (recovery, not repetition), a stack with parallel structure, and a CTA that's earned. None of this exists in the weak version because the weak version skipped research.

## Self-check before you save

Before you call the carousel done, run through these questions:

1. **Could this carousel be about any topic with words swapped?** If yes, you didn't research enough. Add specifics.
2. **Does slide 1 have stakes?** A claim with no stakes is a fact, not a hook.
3. **Does slide 8 actually reframe something?** Or does it just summarize the value slides?
4. **Does slide 9 give actions doable in the next 24 hours?** Or generic advice?
5. **Is the CTA singular?** Pick one ask.
6. **Is the tone right for the topic?** Re-read with the tone matrix in mind.
7. **Did you use any filler words?** Cut them.
8. **Are there exact quotes from sources?** Rewrite in your own words.

If any of these fail, fix before saving.
